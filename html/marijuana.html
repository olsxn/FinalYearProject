Marijuana

Cannabis, popularly known as marijuana or weed, is a psychoactive drug from the Cannabis plant and is used primarily for medical or recreational purposes. THC is the main psychoactive component of cannabis, one of the 483 known compounds in the plant. It can be used by smoking or vaporising it, within food or as an extract. It has mental and physical effects. It causes a high, or stoned feeling and other effects, including a general change in thought and perception, relaxation and an increase in appetite.

(Wikipedia, 2020)

Medical Uses

Medical cannabis refers to the use of cannabis and its cannabinoids to treat or improve symptoms for a wide range of diseases and conditions. These include, but are not limited to:

  • Alzheimer's disease
  • Some forms of cancer
  • Crohn's disease
  • Epilepsy
  • Glaucoma
  • Some mental health conditions like schizophrenia and PTSD

Cannabinoids, the active chemicals in cannabis, are similar to chemicals the body makes that are involved in appetite, memory, movement, and pain. Research suggest that cannabinoids might:

  • Reduce anxiety
  • Reduce inflammation and relieve pain
  • Kill cancer cells and slow tumour growth
  • Stimulate appetite and improve weight gain in people with cancer and AIDS

    • (WebMD, 2018)

It’s best to think of medical cannabis like any other drug compound. Some of the benefits have pretty strong science behind them and others are anecdotal.

Here in the UK, patients have been able to be prescribed medical cannabis by specialist doctors since the 1st of November 2018. The decision to prescribe medical cannabis must be made by a specialist doctor, not a GP. These doctors focus on one field of medicine such as neurology or paediatrics and are listed on the General Medical Council’s specialist register. (GOV.UK, 2018)

Decisions on prescribing cannabis-based products for medicinal use is on a strict case-by-case basis however, so it is extremely uncommon at this moment in time. Just this year, in January, a three year old girl was given a cannabis prescription to treat her severe epilepsy (Medical Cannabis Network, 2020).

CBD

More commonly found in the UK are cannabidiol, commonly known as CBD, products. Cannabidiol is one of the 483 chemical compounds in the cannabis plant. CBD products are legal in the UK as long as they do not contain any detectable levels of controlled substances, such as THC.

CBD has no psychoactive properties and will not give a high. There is no evidence it has any abuse or dependence potential and to date there is no evidence that it is associated with any serious side effects, according to the World Health Organisation.

CBD products boast the same potential advantages as medical marijuana however the only proven health benefit currently is for Lennox-Gastaut syndrome (LGS) and Dravet syndrome (two forms of epilepsy) (Drugs.com, 2019).

Recreational Use

Cannabis is the most popular recreational drug in the UK outside of alcohol and tobacco, making it the most popular illegal drug. The immediate desired affects from consuming cannabis include relaxation, euphoria (the high), and a myriad of other potential affects.

Some side effects include:

  • Decrease in short-term memory
  • Dry mouth
  • Impaired motor skills

    • Reddening of the eyes

(Wikipedia, 2020)

Research shows that 10% of regular cannabis users become dependent on it. The risk is higher if a user starts using it in their teenage years or if they use it every day. As with other addictive drugs, you can develop a tolerance to it. This means you need more to get the same effect.

If you stop using it, you may get withdrawal symptoms such as:

  • Cravings
  • Difficulty sleeping
  • Mood swings
  • Irritability and restlessness

Users who smoke cannabis with tobacco could get addicted to nicotine and risk getting tobacco-related diseases, such as cancer and coronary heart disease.

Regular cannabis use also increases your risk of developing a psychotic illness, such as schizophrenia. A psychotic illness is one where someone has hallucinations and delusions (believing things that are not true). It also increases the risk of a relapse in people who already have schizophrenia or other psychotic illnesses.

(NHS, 2017)

Statistics

In 2014, 93% of drug users in the UK, more than 14 million people, had used marijuana. This had been increasing every since 2008, when 87% had used it. It is often depicted as a gateway drug, and it is true that it is the drug that most drug users use first. It was the first drug that 82% of drug users used. There is no clear evidence, however, that the use of marijuana directly leads to the use of other drugs. In fact, half of marijuana users have never even taken another drug.

(The Guardian, 2014)

In 2018, cannabis was once again the most commonly used drug, with 6.6% of adults having used it in the last year. This was very similar to the two previous years but showed a significant fall compared with use a decade ago (8.2%).

In the same year, men were more than twice as likely to report using cannabis than women were, 9.0% of men compared with 4.2% of women. This remains true for other substances, such as cocaine and ecstasy.

(NHS, 2018)

In 2017-18, there were 10,666 individuals aged 18 to 24 who had started treatment for their problems. The majority of these individuals (5,791 or 54%) had problems with cannabis. Despite this, it only made up for 20% of the total reported problematic substance use, dwarfed by alcohol (49%) and opiate and crack cocaine use.

These statistics show that cannabis is a small part of the much larger problem that is substance abuse and addiction in the UK. It would not be right to say that it leads users onto harder drugs and this is what most education on this drug tries to tell us.
The reality is that cannabis has its own distinct advantages and disadvantages and can affect each person vastly differently. It is down to each individual user to recognise the effect it is having on them and make their own decisions regarding the drug. The undeniable fact is that using cannabis recreationally every day has a clear effect on mental state and can cause many long-lasting problems for any individual.
